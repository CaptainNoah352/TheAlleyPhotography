The story behind the lens

Long before I ever picked up a camera of my own, photography was already part of my life. My grandmother carried a Canon everywhere — on our weekend road trips up to Maine, on family vacations to Florida, through the quiet stretches of small-town Connecticut where I grew up. One of my earliest memories is watching her accidentally shatter a lens on a trip to Disney World and laughing about it the whole drive home. That camera wasn't precious because it was expensive. It was precious because it was always there, turning ordinary days into something worth holding onto.

I grew up surrounded by colonial architecture, antique storefronts, and landscapes that felt like they belonged to another era. There was a timelessness to everything — stone walls, weathered clapboard, light falling through old glass — and without realizing it, that shaped the way I see the world. I've always been drawn to images that feel like they could exist outside of time, moments that carry a kind of quiet weight.

Florida called to me long before I moved here. Those childhood trips with my grandparents planted something — the sprawling wetlands, the prehistoric birds, the golden light that pours through Spanish moss. Now, based in Ocala, I find myself surrounded by one of the most extraordinary ecosystems on Earth. The wildlife, the springs, the coastline — every corner of this state feels alive with something worth documenting.

For me, photography is about preservation. Not in a museum sense, but in a deeply personal one. Every frame is an attempt to hold onto the way a moment looked and felt — to show people the world exactly as I experienced it. The past doesn't stay, but a photograph can make it echo. That's what drives me: turning the fleeting into the lasting, the ordinary into something someone else might feel moved by.

I'm self-taught and still early in this journey, but I'm learning fast and pushing myself with every shoot. My favorite camera is my Canon A-1 — there's nothing quite like the discipline and intention that comes with shooting film. For most of my work, I shoot on a Canon EOS R100, which gives me the flexibility to chase fast-moving wildlife and shifting light. Whether it's 35mm grain or crisp digital, the goal is always the same: make someone stop, look, and feel something.
